Daniel LaBelle has spent years running through the internet.

He runs from people.

He runs from costumed characters.

He runs with the urgency of a man who has apparently never been allowed to leave anywhere normally.

Eventually, the internet reached the only reasonable conclusion:

He keeps going back to Huss Valley.

## What is Huss Valley?

Huss Valley is a fictional location created by internet users around videos of physical comedian Daniel LaBelle.

It is not a real valley.

In the meme, Huss Valley is treated like an absurdly dangerous place where someone is always running, chasing, or being chased.

LaBelle's frantic running videos became the perfect footage for the joke.

The internet simply gave all that running a shared location.

## Where did the Huss Valley meme come from?

The meme started taking shape on TikTok in early July 2026.

An early meme using one of LaBelle's running videos prompted a commenter to joke that he was "hussing."

Then, on July 4, TikTok user @homogenous_banana12 posted a compilation of LaBelle running from costumed characters with a caption asking why he kept coming back to Huss Valley.

According to Know Your Meme, this is the earliest known use of "Huss Valley."

A fake place had been named.

The internet immediately began building the tourism board from hell.

## What does "huss" mean?

There is no official dictionary definition behind the meme.

One popular explanation among commenters is that "huss" sounds like the heavy breathing someone might make while running hard.

That fits the joke unusually well.

If you visit Huss Valley, the meme suggests, you will probably be running.

And if you are running, something is probably behind you.

The ambiguity also helps. "Huss" sounds just strange enough to become its own piece of internet vocabulary without needing a strict meaning.

## Why Daniel LaBelle?

Because the man is always running.

LaBelle describes his work as short physical comedy built around non-verbal communication. Many of his videos turn ordinary situations into exaggerated athletic chaos.

Running is especially useful for Huss Valley memes because his clips already look like scenes from a larger, unexplained disaster.

Remove the original context.

Add a location tag.

Write something about Huss Valley.

Suddenly, a comedy sketch looks like recovered footage from the world's least relaxing national park.

## How did Huss Valley become a whole place?

Once the name existed, creators began treating Huss Valley as if it had its own geography and rules.

Memes warned people about visiting.

Location tags labelled videos as Huss Valley.

Other running clips were pulled into the joke.

Some memes connected Huss Valley with other fictional internet locations, including Folk Valley.

The joke stopped being about one Daniel LaBelle video.

It became shared lore.

Nobody needed to agree on exactly what lived in Huss Valley or why everyone was running. The uncertainty gave creators more room to invent new dangers.

## Why is the meme so funny?

The joke gives a completely unnecessary explanation to years of unrelated videos.

Daniel LaBelle did not create a cinematic universe about a cursed valley.

He made physical comedy videos.

But once someone calls the place Huss Valley, the clips begin to look connected.

Every chase becomes another incident.

Every costume becomes local wildlife.

Every return raises the same question:

Why does this man keep going back?

That is the engine of the meme.

The internet sees a pattern, invents a place, and then acts as if the place had always existed.

## How popular did Huss Valley become?

The format spread quickly across TikTok and Instagram Reels in July 2026.

Know Your Meme documented one July 7 video using a Huss Valley location tag that received more than 2.1 million views in six days.

Other versions moved beyond LaBelle's original running clips, applying the fictional location to bowling and other chaotic situations.

That expansion matters.

A meme becomes much easier to reuse when people no longer need the original video to make the joke work.

Huss Valley had become a setting.

## Is Huss Valley like the Ohio meme?

There is a clear similarity.

The [Ohio meme](/memes/what-does-ohio-mean) turned a real place into internet shorthand for bizarre and impossible events.

Huss Valley works in a related way, except the location itself is fictional.

Both memes create a simple rule:

Something ridiculous happened because of where it happened.

The difference is that Huss Valley grew around running and chase videos, with Daniel LaBelle at the centre of its early lore.

## Why Huss Valley works as a meme

Huss Valley did not need a complicated punchline.

The footage already existed.

Daniel LaBelle was already running.

The internet only had to answer one question:

Where?

Someone said Huss Valley.

And somehow, that was enough to create an entire fictional place where standing still appears to be a terrible survival strategy.
